What is Vertigo?

Understanding the condition that affects your balance and spatial orientation

Vertigo is a sensation of spinning or dizziness that makes a person feel as if they or their surroundings are moving, even when stationary. It is often caused by problems in the inner ear or brain. Vertigo is not a disease but a symptom of various conditions, including vestibular disorders, migraines, or ear infections.

Some individuals may also experience nausea, vomiting, balance problems, and difficulty focusing. Factors such as dehydration, sudden head movements, and stress can trigger or worsen vertigo. Treatment depends on the underlying cause and may include medications, lifestyle changes, and vestibular rehabilitation therapy.

Alternative Names

Peripheral vertigo; Central vertigo; Dizziness; Benign positional vertigo; Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo.

Symptoms

Recognizing the signs and symptoms of vertigo

The main symptom of vertigo is a sensation that you or the room is moving or spinning. This spinning sensation may cause nausea and vomiting. Depending on the cause, other symptoms can include:

  • Problem focusing the eyes
  • Dizziness
  • Hearing loss in one or both ears
  • Loss of balance (may cause falls)
  • Ringing in the ears
  • Nausea and vomiting, leading to loss of body fluids

If you have vertigo due to problems in the brain (central vertigo), you may have other symptoms, including:

  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Double vision
  • Eye movement problems
  • Facial paralysis
  • Slurred speech
  • Weakness of the limbs
Possible Complications

Vertigo can cause falls, which may result in bone fractures (broken bones) or other injuries. Vertigo can also interfere with your quality of life and hinder your ability to drive or go to work.

Types of Vertigo

Understanding the different classifications of vertigo

Peripheral Vertigo

Peripheral vertigo is the most common type of vertigo and is caused by problems in the inner ear, which controls our balance. It presents with symptoms such as spinning sensation, sweating, nausea, vomiting, and ear problems. If peripheral vertigo is due to an infection in the inner ear, there can be pain or feeling of fullness in the ear.

Causes of Peripheral Vertigo

Peripheral vertigo is mostly caused by problems in the inner ear, which helps maintain balance. Common causes include:

  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Tiny calcium crystals in the ear become dislodged, sending false signals to the brain
  • Ménière's disease - Caused by fluid buildup in the inner ear, leading to vertigo, hearing loss, and ringing in the ears
  • Vestibular neuritis - Inflammation of the vestibular nerve often due to a viral infection
  • Labyrinthitis - An inner ear infection that affects both balance and hearing
  • Head injuries - Can damage inner ear structures

Treatment of Peripheral Vertigo

Peripheral vertigo can be treated using various approaches:

  • Medications such as antibiotics (for infections), antihistamines, and anti-nausea drugs
  • The Epley maneuver and Brandt-Daroff exercises for BPPV
  • Vestibular rehabilitation therapy
  • Lifestyle changes including low salt diet, avoiding alcohol, and stress management

Central Vertigo

Central vertigo is caused by issues in the brain, mainly in areas like the cerebellum and brainstem that control balance. It tends to be more persistent and less responsive to treatment than peripheral vertigo.

Causes of Central Vertigo

Central vertigo is caused by problems affecting the brain. Common causes include:

  • Stroke - Affects blood flow to the brain, leading to vertigo, double vision, and slurred speech
  • Multiple sclerosis (MS) - A condition that damages nerve coverings, affecting balance
  • Migraines - Especially vestibular migraines, which can trigger episodes of dizziness and nausea
  • Brain tumors - Pressing on balance centers can cause persistent vertigo
  • Traumatic brain injury (TBI) - From accidents or falls

Treatment of Central Vertigo

Central vertigo can only be managed by diagnosing and treating the root cause:

  • Treating the underlying condition (stroke, MS, tumors)
  • Prophylactic migraine medications for migraine-related vertigo
  • Medications for symptom management (nausea, dizziness)
  • Vestibular rehabilitation in some cases

New Type of Vertigo

Apart from peripheral and central vertigo, neurologists recently identified a new type of vertigo which had no known cause but responds to treatment. This condition is known as recurrent spontaneous vertigo with head-shaking nystagmus.

Patients with this condition experience vertigo attacks accompanied by headaches, nausea, and vomiting. These patients are also more likely to experience severe motion sickness than patients with other types of vertigo.

Research Note

This newly identified type of vertigo is still being studied, and treatment protocols are being developed. If you experience symptoms that don't fit the traditional categories, consult with a neurologist who specializes in vestibular disorders.

Stages of Vertigo

Understanding the progression and severity levels of vertigo

Vertigo is generally classified into three stages based on the severity and impact of symptoms:

Mild Vertigo

Mild vertigo is the initial and least severe stage of vertigo, where a person experiences a gentle sensation of dizziness or a slight spinning feeling. These episodes are usually brief and may occur when changing positions, such as getting out of bed, bending down, or turning the head quickly.

People with mild vertigo often feel a sense of imbalance but are usually able to continue with their normal activities. Factors like stress, anxiety, dehydration, lack of sleep, or sudden head movements can sometimes trigger these mild symptoms.

Managing mild vertigo often involves simple lifestyle adjustments such as avoiding sudden movements, staying well-hydrated, and practicing relaxation techniques to reduce stress. If mild vertigo occurs regularly, consulting a healthcare professional is advised to prevent it from progressing to moderate or severe stages.

Moderate Vertigo

Moderate vertigo is the next stage where dizziness and spinning sensations become more noticeable and persistent. People with moderate vertigo may experience a stronger feeling of movement that lasts longer and interferes with daily activities like walking, working, or driving.

Along with spinning, other symptoms such as nausea, unsteadiness, and difficulty focusing may appear. Head movements or sudden position changes can easily trigger these episodes. Unlike mild vertigo, moderate vertigo can be more distressing and may cause people to sit or lie down until the sensation passes.

Managing moderate vertigo usually requires medical attention to identify the cause and begin treatment. Doctors may recommend vestibular rehabilitation exercises, medications to reduce dizziness and nausea, and specific maneuvers like the Epley maneuver in cases like BPPV.

Severe Vertigo

Severe vertigo is the most intense stage, where the spinning sensation becomes overwhelming and constant. People may feel like the entire environment is moving rapidly, making it impossible to stand, walk, or even sit upright without assistance.

This stage is often accompanied by severe nausea, vomiting, loss of balance, and a strong fear of falling. Simple movements, such as turning the head or getting up from bed, can worsen the symptoms. In some cases, severe vertigo may also be linked with hearing problems, vision disturbances, and sweating.

Managing severe vertigo requires immediate medical care. Doctors may prescribe strong anti-vertigo medications and anti-nausea drugs to control symptoms. In hospital settings, patients may receive fluids if they are dehydrated due to vomiting. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent complications and improve quality of life.

Precautions for Vertigo

Essential safety measures and lifestyle adjustments to manage vertigo

Taking the right precautions can help reduce the frequency and severity of vertigo attacks. Here are important measures to consider:

Home Safety

  • Remove tripping hazards like loose rugs and clutter
  • Install handrails in bathrooms and along staircases
  • Use proper lighting, especially at night
  • Consider using a walking aid if balance is severely affected

Movement Precautions

  • Avoid sudden head movements or quick changes in position
  • Get up slowly from bed or chairs
  • Sit down immediately if you feel dizzy
  • Avoid activities like driving or climbing until vertigo improves

Lifestyle Adjustments

  • Limit salt intake, caffeine, alcohol, and tobacco
  • Stay well-hydrated
  • Get enough sleep and manage stress
  • Practice stress-reduction techniques like yoga or meditation
  • Follow prescribed treatments and attend vestibular rehabilitation therapy
When to Seek Medical Help

Contact your healthcare provider immediately if you experience new or severe vertigo symptoms, especially if accompanied by headache, difficulty speaking, weakness, or changes in vision. These could indicate a more serious condition requiring prompt medical attention.
